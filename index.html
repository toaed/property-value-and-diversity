Dallas County Market Analysis

Property Value, Density & Use Diversity in Dallas County

This report examines land value, density, and use patterns across Dallas County within the DFW market, using parcel-level data to illustrate how value is distributed geographically.

01

Value Concentration in Dallas County

Figure 1A shows assessed value per acre at the parcel level across Dallas County. Each parcel is colored based on its relative property value per acre of parceled land: red indicates the highest values, green the lowest, and yellow represents the median.

The highest concentrations of value are centered in Downtown and Uptown Dallas, with values extending primarily north along the Dallas North Tollway. Additional clusters of higher value appear in Irving, Richardson, and the Bishop Arts/Oak Cliff area, while values generally decrease moving outward toward the edges of the county.

Figure 1A — Value per Acre by Parcel
Assessed value per acre at the parcel level. Red = highest value; Green = lowest; Yellow = median.

Figure 1B presents assessed value per acre aggregated at the ZIP code level across Dallas County in an interactive format.

The highest concentration of value is located in Downtown Dallas, exceeding $13 million per acre, followed by Uptown at approximately $11 million per acre. Values decline sharply moving west, south, and east, where adjacent ZIP codes fall below $1.5 million per acre. In contrast, the decline moving north is more gradual, with both ZIP codes in Addison, over 15 miles north of downtown, maintaining values above $2 million per acre.

Figure 1C ranks cities in Dallas County by assessed value per acre of parceled land. Highland Park has the highest concentration of value at approximately $12.9 million per acre, followed by University Park at $12.4 million per acre. Addison ranks third and is the only other city exceeding $2 million per acre, at approximately $2.8 million per acre.

02

Building Area as a Share of Developable Land

Development density is a natural starting point for understanding differences in value concentration, as higher density allows more property value to be supported on each acre of land. In this report, density is measured as the ratio of total building area to total parcel land area.

Figure 2 ranks cities in Dallas County by this density measure. University Park is the most dense, with total building area equal to approximately 42% of its parcel land area, followed by Addison at 37%. Density declines across the remaining cities, with many falling between 10% and 25%. At the lower end, cities such as Ferris exhibit very low density, with total building area representing only 0.3% of total land area.

03

Value per Acre and Density by Land Use Category

Differences in value and density across cities are partly driven by the types of land uses that predominate in each location.

Figures 3A and 3B show value per acre and density by land use category across Dallas County. Data centers have the highest value per acre, followed by hospitals and multifamily uses. In contrast, when measured by density, hospitals and multifamily lead, reflecting a higher concentration of building area relative to land.

04

Distribution of Land Uses across Dallas County Cities

Figure 4A shows the dominant land use by parcel across Dallas County, illustrating where different uses concentrate geographically.

Industrial activity is visibly clustered in several areas, including Coppell north of DFW Airport, along the I-35 corridor through Carrollton, Farmers Branch, and Dallas, and in Garland and Mesquite. Residential areas are widespread and are often accompanied by adjacent retail nodes, particularly in North Dallas and the Park Cities.

In terms of use diversity, Uptown Dallas naturally shows a clear concentration of multiple uses in close proximity. Addison is the only other area where mixed-use patterns are evident, with a combination of industrial, retail, office, multifamily, and airport-related uses appearing within a compact area.

Figure 4A — Dominant Use by Parcel
Dominant land use category by parcel across Dallas County.

Figure 4B ranks cities in Dallas County by land use diversity. Addison has the highest level of diversity, with nine different uses each representing more than 5% of total land area. No other city exceeds five uses above this threshold.

This pattern holds when the metric is changed to share of total building area or total property value, with Addison consistently maintaining more uses above 5% than any other city in Dallas County.

Recent reports by JLL, McKinsey, and CBRE on office and retail performance note that mixed-use environments tend to outperform single-use counterparts. By virtue of its size and distribution of uses, Addison functions as a built-in mixed-use structure.
